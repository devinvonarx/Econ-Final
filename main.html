History


The crossword puzzle industry started in 1913 with the publication of the first newspaper crossword puzzle. By 1924 it had exploded in popularity culminating in a crossword puzzle book published as a small print run by Simon and Schuster, the popularity of the book launched them as a major publishing company. Crosswords continued to expand in popularity and by 1941 every major newspaper in America published a crossword puzzle except for the New York Times. When the Times finally gave in to the demand and began publishing a puzzle in 1942 they hired Simon and Schusterâ€™s puzzle editor, Margaret Farrar, to manage it.


The early economic history of newspaper crosswords is an economic model based on small part time puzzle makers submitting puzzles for small fees which are edited and then published. For the New York Times this model remains mostly unchanged, although puzzle submitters are paid more today and the puzzle editor has a staff of three to help out. For many other papers, however, the model has shifted. They now buy crosswords from a syndicate rather than individual submissions. This means that many papers now offer the same puzzle meaning the puzzle is less of a unique profit driver.

Consumer


According to a study conducted by the Census Bureau, in 2010 13.2% of adult Americans played crossword puzzle in the last 12 months. 5.6% played at least two times a week. 1.4% played only once a week. 1.2% played only two to three times a month. 1.2% played only once a month.

Producer

The main company that produces crossword puzzles is The New York Times is the main producer. The company has had only had four crossword puzzle editors. The current one is Will Shortz, a man with a collection of more than 25,000 puzzle books and magazines.

The New York Times pays much more for crossword puzzles than anyone else. This causes their puzzles to be of supieror quality, giving them a monopoly for high quality crossword puzzles.

His work has been published in The New York Times, The Washington Post, The Wall Street Journal, The Boston Globe, by the Los Angeles Times Syndicate, and The Onion. Fun Fact:

According to Brendan Emmett Quigley, there are around 11 people who make crossword puzzles as a full time job.

Future of the Industry:

I think we'll see more mini-puzzles like Wordle take off. There is so much content fighting for people's time nowadays, and bite-sized puzzles will probably go on a bit of a resurgence. But on the whole, I think the crossword is very healthy. The English language is constantly changing, so there will always be words to play with. Plus it's a classic now. It's like one of the first things you think of when you hear "puzzle."

-Quigley

The crossword puzzle industry makes zero economic profit in the long run. The market is monopolistic competition, meaning there are low barriers to entry for the crossword puzzle industry.

However, The New York Times makes a positive economic profit because it has name recognition and thus can charge above the industry price.
